Libby

Innovating the book exploration experience for digital library users

About the Project

Overview

Libby, a public library app for borrowing and perusing library eBooks and audiobooks, needed a revamp of its clunky book exploration experience. After doing research into user behavior trends and points of confusion, I innovated features that served all observed user archetypes via a straightforward interface that exchanges frills for clarity.

Research

Survey

Who are the users and what do they want? In order to get a picture of users' expectations and desires, I asked 5 participants questions that ranged from general (such as asking about their motivations for borrowing) to specific (such as asking about what types of information they use to decide whether to borrow a title).

Usability Tests

A round of usability testing enabled me to directly determine points of confusion for users in the current site.

Heuristics Analysis

Pain points were also discovered via a heuristics analysis using established guidelines for web design (I used the Web Accessibility Guidelines) and science-backed heuristics like Hick’s Law.

Insights + Define

User Behavior Insights

I found that all the users behaved in 1 of 3 ways. Note that the following archetypes should not be used to classify users; rather, these are behavior patterns and motivations that the same user may experience at different instances.

3 archetypes for user behavior
Picky Reader
  • They know what types of books & search for books that fit a specific combinations of genres.
  • They are frustrated because the app does not have robust capabilities for detailed searching and filtering.
Searchers
  • They had a specific book in mind and went straight for the search bar.
Recommendation Seeker
  • They looked for recommendations such as library-curated guides.

Since the goal was to improve the book exploration process, I wanted to find out users’ inclinations when it comes to preferences. In hindsight, the result was obvious: each person was unique.

Preference styles are extremely unique to each person
  • Some stuck with 1 genre, some with many. Some were exploratory while others were overwhelmed by choice. So, the solution would not be one-size-fits-all.

Website Insights

The website also had existing issues that confused users.

Existing points of confusion hindered book exploration rather than supporting it
Mystery icons & site structure
  • Mystery icons with no descriptive text confused users—especially as the icons are unique to Libby rather than commonly seen, standardized icons.
  • The Teen Guides page looks like 1 list of books, but it’s actually several. This confused users.
Too Much Info
  • Users are hindered by information overload such as being presented with unnecessary options and redundant listings.
Listings Miss the Mark
  • Book listings lack the information critical to users. Users depend on user reviews, genres, and synopsis, but these are missing, lacking, or hidden.

Define Design Needs

In sum, how might we serve Picky Readers and enhance the experience of Searchers and Recommendation Seekers? How can we serve the great variety in book preferences? How might we resolve points of confusion like mystery icons & site structure, information overload, and listings that lack or hide user-desired information?

Design

Ideation

Brainstorming potential features to address the user behavior insights, I focused on ideas that serve the 3 archetypes, avoid choice overload, enable customization to fit unique preferences, and immediately engage users. As for the app design’s current points of confusion, I focused on a balance of providing user-desired information without overwhelming users.

The Solutions

After narrowing down my ideas, I decided on 4 changes to serve Picky Readers and enhance the experience of Searchers and Recommendation Seekers.

Features catered to each user behavior archtype
User Curated Collections

User-curated collections allow users to browse and share titles that fit their unique and specific preferences.

  • Picky Readers can share recommendations with like-minded fellows, and search for titles that aren’t fully described with the standard genre tags.
  • Recommendation Seekers get recommendations.
Personalized Suggestions

Users are suggested titles based on their likes and dislikes.

  • Picky Readers get suggestions that fit their unique tastes, while Recommendation Seekers get recommendations.
  • With a simple point of entry, users can get started quick without feeling overwhelmed.
Fine-Grain Filters

Users can apply a detailed combination of filters.

  • A filter search features makes it easy to find a specific filter without wading through the long list of genres and subjects.
  • Perfect for Picky Readers
Quick Search
  • Adding a quick search to the main page serves the Searchers behavior archetype.
Removed existing points of confusion that hindered book exploration rather than supporting it
De-mystify Icons & Site Structure
  • Added labels and removed non-descriptive icons.
  • Clarified site structure with a tab layout enabling users to switch between multiple lists.
Prune Distracting Info
  • Combined eBook and audiobook listings to remove redundancy
  • Collapsed librarian-curated recommendation list into 1 clickable item.
Listings Show User-Desired Info
  • Showed genres, user ratings, and synopsis in Iist view so users can quickly make a preliminary decision without tapping into each individual listing.

Reflection

This project highlighted to me the importance of testing with real users. The obstacles and opportunities I discovered during research and analysis were very different from what I had personally expected. Testing ensured that I was addressing real issues.

If I had more time, I would like to create a prototype and conduct another round of usability testing. I also would also have liked to explore more facets of the app such as the dashboard, managing holds/borrows, and so on.
